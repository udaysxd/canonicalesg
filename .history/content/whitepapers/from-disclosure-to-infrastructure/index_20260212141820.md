# Executive Summary

Financial reporting achieved global trust not merely through accounting standards, but through structural discipline.

Across jurisdictions applying IFRS or US GAAP, companies present:

- A Balance Sheet  
- An Income Statement  
- A Cash Flow Statement  

The format is recognisable, structured, comparable, and machine-readable (via XBRL).

Sustainability reporting has reached regulatory convergence at the level of standards — notably through:

- International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB) — IFRS S1 and S2  
  https://www.ifrs.org/groups/international-sustainability-standards-board/
- European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS) under the EU CSRD  
  https://finance.ec.europa.eu/capital-markets-union-and-financial-markets/company-reporting-and-auditing/company-reporting/corporate-sustainability-reporting_en
- India’s BRSR Core (SEBI)  
  https://www.sebi.gov.in
- GHG Protocol  
  https://ghgprotocol.org

However, there remains no globally accepted standard reporting format for sustainability disclosures.

This absence of structural presentation discipline is now the primary maturity gap.

The objective of this paper is precise:

> To propose the standardisation of sustainability reporting format through structured sustainability statements supported by a neutral interoperability layer.

Not a new framework.  
Not a new regulator.  
A structural reform.

# 2. Economic Consequences of Structural Fragmentation

Fragmented infrastructure generates measurable financial cost.

## Direct Costs

- 57% of large firms spend >$1m annually on ESG reporting (PwC 2024)  
- External rating reconciliation fees up to $500k annually  
- Duplicate data preparation across ISSB, ESRS, GRI, CDP  
- 83% of firms expect costs to rise due to framework fragmentation (Business at OECD)

## Indirect Costs

- Higher cost of capital linked to rating divergence  
- 2–5% stock underperformance following ESG controversies  
- 2.3x reputational amplification vs traditional scandals  
- Reduced investor confidence due to rating inconsistency (Berg, Koelbel & Rigobon — MIT “Aggregate Confusion”)

The maturity gap is economically measurable.

# 4. Lessons from Financial Reporting Reform

Financial reporting stabilised when diversity was organised into structure.

| Statement        | Purpose       |
|------------------|--------------|
| Balance Sheet    | Position     |
| Income Statement | Performance  |
| Cash Flow        | Liquidity    |

These statements:

- Standardised presentation  
- Enabled XBRL tagging (SEC EDGAR / ESMA ESEF)  
  https://www.sec.gov/structureddata  
  https://www.esma.europa.eu  
- Reduced interpretive ambiguity  
- Created investor-grade comparability  

ESG lacks equivalent structured statements.
