# Building a Unified ESG Reporting Foundation for the GCC  
## A Practical Infrastructure Proposal for Regional Alignment  

---

## Executive Summary

Sustainability reporting across the Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC) is evolving within a dynamic global regulatory environment. Member states have introduced ESG-related guidance, capital market expectations, and sustainable finance initiatives aligned with international frameworks. However, implementation structures remain at varying stages of development, and regional coordination mechanisms are still emerging.

Across the GCC, sustainability disclosure is increasingly referenced within national transformation strategies, financial market oversight, and climate commitments. Regulatory bodies, stock exchanges, sovereign wealth funds, and financial free zones are progressively integrating ESG considerations into governance and reporting expectations. While important foundations have been established, the operational architecture for consistent, cross-border implementation remains heterogeneous.

At the same time, global sustainability reporting standards — particularly those issued by the International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB) — are gaining rapid international adoption. As capital markets converge around common disclosure baselines, GCC jurisdictions face a strategic opportunity: not merely to align with global standards individually, but to strengthen regional coherence in how those standards are implemented.

Global climate commitments under the Paris Agreement (2015), reinforced through successive Conference of the Parties (COP) processes — including COP26 (Glasgow), COP27 (Sharm El-Sheikh), and COP28 (Dubai) — have accelerated the translation of climate ambition into regulatory and financial disclosure expectations. At the same time, the establishment of the International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB) under the IFRS Foundation has created a globally coordinated sustainability disclosure baseline for capital markets.

Across the GCC, member states are progressively integrating sustainability disclosure into financial regulation, capital market supervision, and national transformation strategies.

The United Arab Emirates has embedded sustainability within federal economic policy, introduced ESG disclosure guidance through the Securities and Commodities Authority (SCA), and advanced sustainable finance frameworks within ADGM and DIFC. The UAE Net Zero 2050 Strategic Initiative further reinforces long-term climate disclosure expectations.

The Kingdom of Saudi Arabia has incorporated ESG within Vision 2030 reforms, with ESG disclosure guidance issued by the Capital Market Authority (CMA), sustainability initiatives advanced through the Saudi Exchange (Tadawul), and ESG integration embedded within the Public Investment Fund (PIF).

Qatar, Bahrain, Oman, and Kuwait have each introduced ESG or sustainability disclosure guidance through their respective capital market regulators and stock exchanges. Sustainability transparency is increasingly expected within annual reporting cycles, with progressive alignment toward international standards including IFRS Sustainability Disclosure Standards (ISSB), GRI, and TCFD-aligned climate frameworks.

While regulatory direction across the GCC demonstrates convergence toward international standards, implementation architectures remain heterogeneous.

Global standards define what should be disclosed.  
They do not define how sustainability data should be structurally modelled, how disclosure meaning should remain stable across frameworks, or how jurisdiction-specific overlays should be applied without duplicating underlying data.

As disclosure requirements expand, implementation complexity increases. Without shared structural infrastructure:

- interpretation may diverge across jurisdictions,
- advisory and software providers may embed framework semantics directly into data models,
- multinational GCC entities may duplicate reporting logic across regimes,
- cross-border comparability may weaken despite standard alignment.

The next stage of regional sustainability leadership is therefore not the creation of new standards, but the harmonisation of implementation beneath existing standards.

This white paper proposes the development of a neutral, technical sustainability reporting infrastructure layer designed to:

- preserve alignment with global standards,
- reduce implementation divergence,
- enhance comparability across GCC capital markets,
- strengthen regional regulatory coherence,
- and support long-term institutional stability.

The proposal does not introduce new disclosure requirements.  
It does not replace ISSB, GRI, or national regulation.  

It provides shared structural infrastructure beneath them — enabling each GCC jurisdiction to retain regulatory sovereignty while benefiting from regional coherence and implementation stability.

The evolution of sustainability reporting within the GCC must be understood within a broader global institutional transformation.

IFRS S1 (General Requirements for Disclosure of Sustainability-related Financial Information) and IFRS S2 (Climate-related Disclosures) are increasingly referenced by regulators worldwide.

GCC jurisdictions are aligning with this global baseline while preserving national economic priorities and legal structures.

## 2. The Implementation Infrastructure Gap

International sustainability standards have achieved significant conceptual convergence over the past decade. Frameworks such as the IFRS Sustainability Disclosure Standards (ISSB), the Global Reporting Initiative (GRI), and climate-related disclosure architectures influenced by the TCFD provide structured disclosure requirements, thematic topic groupings, and materiality-based reporting logic.

These frameworks define what should be disclosed and, increasingly, why such disclosures are relevant to capital markets, regulators, and stakeholders.

However, they do not prescribe how sustainability information should be structurally modelled within enterprise systems, how disclosure meaning should be stabilised across multiple frameworks, or how regulatory overlays should be applied without duplicating underlying data.

In particular, global standards do not define:

- A canonical data architecture for sustainability metrics and assertions  
- Stable, framework-independent semantic identifiers for disclosure meaning  
- A structured separation between raw sustainability data, disclosure intent, and jurisdictional regulatory obligation  
- A reusable modelling layer capable of supporting multiple reporting regimes simultaneously  

As a result, implementation practices vary significantly across jurisdictions, advisory firms, and software platforms.

In the absence of shared technical reference infrastructure:

- Organisations frequently remodel sustainability data for each reporting framework adopted.  
- Regulatory updates may require costly structural redesign of reporting systems rather than targeted interpretive adjustments.  
- Interpretive logic becomes embedded directly into proprietary software architectures, reducing transparency and portability.  
- Cross-border comparability is weakened, even where formal alignment with global standards exists.  
- Long-term interoperability between GCC markets and international capital systems becomes dependent on vendor-specific implementation approaches.  

This structural fragmentation does not arise from divergence in standards themselves. Rather, it emerges from the absence of a neutral implementation layer beneath them.

As sustainability disclosure regimes evolve within the GCC, the policy question gradually shifts from which standards to reference toward how those standards are operationalised consistently, efficiently, and in a manner that supports cross-border comparability.

The implementation infrastructure gap therefore represents a technical and institutional opportunity.

By establishing a shared, neutral reference layer for modelling sustainability data and disclosure semantics — without altering global standards or national regulatory authority — GCC jurisdictions could reduce duplication, enhance comparability, and strengthen long-term regulatory coherence.

Such an approach would support global alignment while preserving sovereign regulatory discretion.

## 3. A Neutral Implementation Foundation

A unified ESG reporting foundation for the GCC would introduce a structured, layered implementation architecture designed to enhance consistency while preserving regulatory autonomy.

Such an architecture would:

- Distinguish sustainability data from disclosure formatting requirements  
- Separate stable disclosure meaning from jurisdiction-specific regulatory obligations  
- Allow global or national reporting frameworks to be applied as transparent interpretive overlays  
- Preserve auditability, version control, and traceability across reporting cycles  
- Enable jurisdictional variation without redefining underlying semantic concepts  

Under this approach, regulatory authorities retain full discretion over disclosure requirements, scope, materiality thresholds, and enforcement mechanisms. The proposed foundation operates beneath these determinations as shared technical infrastructure.

Importantly, this foundation would not:

- Replace or reinterpret ISSB, GRI, or other recognised standards  
- Override national regulatory authority or capital market governance  
- Determine materiality assessments or compliance outcomes  
- Function as a certification or assurance mechanism  

Rather, it would provide structured consistency beneath existing regulatory frameworks, enabling jurisdictions to align implementation practices while maintaining sovereign policy control.

The objective is not regulatory centralisation, but implementation coherence.

## 4. Proposed Layered Architecture

To support coherent and interoperable sustainability reporting across GCC jurisdictions, this paper proposes a structured, three-layer implementation architecture.

The objective of this architecture is not to redefine regulatory obligations, but to provide a durable structural foundation beneath them. By clearly separating data, meaning, and regulatory interpretation, the region can enhance comparability, reduce duplication, and preserve institutional flexibility as standards evolve.

---

### Layer 1 — Core Sustainability Data Model

The first layer establishes a neutral, framework-independent structure for modelling sustainability information at its source.

This layer captures factual and evidentiary elements, including:

- Quantitative metrics (e.g., emissions, energy use, water withdrawal)
- Intensity metrics and calculated indicators
- Targets and baseline references
- Transition trajectories and forward-looking pathways
- Organisational and operational boundaries
- Value chain scope declarations
- Geospatial boundaries and facility-level attributes
- Methodologies applied in calculation or estimation
- Supporting evidence and assurance references

The Core Sustainability Data Model represents assertions of fact and structured information. It is independent of:

- Reporting standards
- Jurisdictional mandates
- Disclosure sequencing or formatting requirements
- Compliance logic

It does not determine materiality.
It does not define reporting thresholds.
It does not encode regulatory interpretation.

Its sole purpose is to ensure that sustainability data is modelled in a consistent, traceable, and reusable manner.

By stabilising the data architecture, regulatory evolution does not require repeated structural redesign. This enhances institutional continuity and reduces implementation cost over time.

---

### Layer 2 — Disclosure Intent Layer

The second layer introduces a stable semantic framework that defines what an organisation intends to communicate externally.

This layer establishes uniquely identified disclosure concepts that are independent of:

- Framework numbering systems
- Jurisdiction-specific obligations
- Report templates or questionnaire formats
- Presentation sequencing

For example, concepts such as:

- Gross Scope 1 emissions
- Existence of a climate transition plan
- Progress against a target
- Description of governance oversight

would be defined once as durable semantic units.

Each disclosure intent:

- References underlying data elements from Layer 1
- Declares boundary and temporal applicability
- Remains stable even if reporting frameworks evolve
- Can be consumed by multiple standards simultaneously

This layer prevents duplication of meaning across frameworks. Instead of redefining similar disclosures under different regulatory systems, a single semantic anchor can serve multiple interpretive contexts.

The Disclosure Intent Layer therefore provides long-term semantic stability across cross-border capital markets.

---

### Layer 3 — Framework and Jurisdiction Mapping Layer

The third layer documents how disclosure meaning is interpreted within specific reporting frameworks and regulatory regimes.

This layer does not redefine disclosure concepts. Rather, it:

- Maps disclosure intents to ISSB, GRI, or other recognised framework requirements
- Documents jurisdiction-specific reporting overlays
- Records assumptions, interpretive conditions, and scope limitations
- Preserves professional judgement transparently
- Evolves independently of the underlying data and semantic layers

Crucially, this layer ensures that:

- Regulatory authorities retain full policy control
- National regulators define scope, enforcement, and compliance thresholds
- Global standards remain authoritative within their domain
- Interpretive logic is documented rather than embedded invisibly within software systems

By isolating regulatory interpretation at a distinct layer, jurisdictions can update requirements without destabilising data architecture or disclosure meaning.

---

### Institutional Benefits of the Layered Approach

A structured layered architecture would enable GCC jurisdictions to:

- Maintain sovereign regulatory authority while aligning structurally
- Reduce cross-border reporting divergence
- Lower compliance and advisory duplication for multinational entities
- Improve audit traceability and comparability
- Support capital market integration
- Enhance long-term regulatory adaptability

The architecture does not introduce a new reporting standard.

It provides shared implementation infrastructure beneath existing standards — allowing policy frameworks to remain distinct while ensuring structural coherence across the region.

## 6. Illustrative UAE Operationalisation Model  
### From Policy Requirement to Structured Implementation

While Section 4 described the conceptual layered architecture, this section illustrates how such an infrastructure could operate in practice within the United Arab Emirates regulatory environment, using actual disclosure standards and supervisory structures.

This section focuses on operational translation rather than architectural abstraction.

---

### 6.1 Regulatory Context in the UAE

The UAE regulatory landscape currently includes:

- **Securities and Commodities Authority (SCA)** ESG disclosure guidance for listed companies  
- **ADGM Financial Services Regulatory Authority (FSRA)** sustainable finance frameworks  
- **Dubai Financial Services Authority (DFSA) within DIFC**, integrating sustainability risk and disclosure into rulebooks  
- Alignment trends toward **IFRS Sustainability Disclosure Standards (ISSB – IFRS S1 and IFRS S2)**  
- Continued reference to **GRI Standards** for broader sustainability topics  
- Climate expectations aligned with UAE Net Zero 2050  

These instruments define *what* must be disclosed or expected.  
They do not define how disclosures should be technically structured beneath the surface.

---

### 6.2 Example: Climate Disclosure Implementation

Consider climate reporting requirements aligned with IFRS S2.

#### IFRS S2 requires disclosure of:

- Governance of climate-related risks and opportunities  
- Strategy and transition planning  
- Climate-related risk management processes  
- Scope 1, Scope 2, and Scope 3 greenhouse gas emissions  
- Targets and progress  

Under a structured implementation foundation, this would translate into:

---

### A. Core Sustainability Data Layer (Factual Modelling)

Entities would model:

- **Metric: Scope 1 emissions (absolute, tCO₂e)**
- **Metric: Scope 2 emissions (location-based and market-based)**
- **Metric: Scope 3 emissions by category**
- **Energy consumption by source**
- **Baseline year**
- **Target reduction percentage**
- **Trajectory pathway**
- **Methodology reference (e.g., GHG Protocol)**
- **Boundary: operational control or financial control**
- **Geospatial attributes (facility-level, country-level)**
- **Evidence references and assurance status**

No reference to IFRS, SCA, ADGM, or DIFC appears at this level.

This layer simply captures verified sustainability information in a stable, structured format.

---

### B. Disclosure Intent Layer (Stable Semantic Anchors)

Above the data, semantic disclosure identifiers would define reusable concepts such as:

- **CDI-CLIM-01 — Gross Scope 1 Emissions**
- **CDI-CLIM-02 — Gross Scope 2 Emissions**
- **CDI-CLIM-07 — Climate Transition Plan**
- **CDI-CLIM-10 — Climate Risk Management Process**
- **CDI-CLIM-14 — Climate Targets and Progress**

Each CDI:

- References underlying data elements
- Declares boundary and temporal applicability
- Remains stable regardless of framework format
- Can be reused across IFRS S2, GRI 305, and any UAE domestic overlay

This layer ensures that “Scope 1 emissions” is defined once and reused, rather than reinterpreted separately per framework.

---

### C. Framework Mapping Layer (CMP – Canonical Mapping Packs)

The interpretive layer would then document how UAE-aligned frameworks consume those CDIs.

For example:

**IFRS S2 Mapping**

- IFRS S2 ¶21–29 → CDI-CLIM-01, CDI-CLIM-02, CDI-CLIM-03  
- IFRS S2 Governance section → CDI-GOVR-02  
- IFRS S2 Targets disclosure → CDI-CLIM-14  

**GRI 305 Mapping**

- GRI 305-1 → CDI-CLIM-01  
- GRI 305-2 → CDI-CLIM-02  
- GRI 305-3 → CDI-CLIM-03  

**UAE Regulatory Overlay**

If SCA requires additional narrative explanation regarding methodology or local reporting format:

- The regulatory overlay would reference the same CDIs
- Introduce formatting or timing requirements
- Preserve the original semantic meaning

The mapping layer explicitly documents interpretive choices.

It does not alter data.
It does not redefine disclosure meaning.
It does not override regulatory authority.

---

### 6.3 EU Taxonomy or Regional Green Finance Alignment (Future Scenario)

If the UAE were to introduce taxonomy-style classification (e.g., green activity eligibility), this could be layered through:

- **CDI-ECON-02 — Revenue by Activity**
- **CDI-CLIM-04 — Lifecycle Emissions Intensity**
- **CDI-MATR-04 — Circular Material Inputs**

A regulatory screening logic could reference these CDIs without duplicating data models.

This avoids parallel “taxonomy spreadsheets” disconnected from core sustainability data.

---

### 6.4 Supervisory and Audit Benefits

Under this operational model:

- Regulators review disclosures mapped through documented CMPs.
- Auditors trace each disclosed figure back to:
  - Canonical metric
  - Boundary declaration
  - Methodology reference
  - Evidence linkage
- Regulatory updates (e.g., IFRS amendments) require updating only the mapping layer, not underlying systems.

This materially reduces supervisory fragmentation.

---

### 6.5 Cross-Zone Integration (ADGM and DIFC)

Because ADGM and DIFC operate under internationally aligned regulatory frameworks:

- The same CDIs can be consumed by multiple rulebooks.
- Mapping packs can document differences transparently.
- Cross-listing entities avoid rebuilding disclosure logic per zone.

This strengthens the UAE’s position as a harmonised financial hub.

---

### 6.6 Strategic Institutional Outcome

Operationally, this approach would:

- Reduce duplication between ISSB-aligned reporting and domestic supervisory guidance
- Prevent software vendors from embedding framework semantics directly into proprietary systems
- Preserve regulatory sovereignty while enhancing structural coherence
- Improve capital market comparability
- Support long-term policy flexibility as sustainability regulation matures

This section demonstrates that the proposed foundation is not theoretical.

It can be applied directly to real regulatory instruments currently active in the UAE, without replacing standards, altering obligations, or introducing new compliance burdens.

It provides structural clarity beneath existing law.

## 7. Institutional Governance and Regional Coordination Considerations

The introduction of a neutral implementation infrastructure requires careful institutional design.  
Such infrastructure should reinforce — not disrupt — existing regulatory authority and sovereign policy control.

This section outlines governance considerations that may support measured and coordinated adoption across the GCC.

---

### 7.1 National Stewardship Models

A member state may elect to engage with the proposed infrastructure through several possible governance approaches:

- **Regulator-Endorsed Reference Model**  
  The infrastructure may be recognised as a recommended technical baseline for sustainability data structuring, without altering disclosure obligations.

- **Technical Guidance Annex**  
  Regulators may reference the infrastructure within non-binding implementation guidance accompanying ESG disclosure rules.

- **Supervisory Interoperability Layer**  
  Supervisory authorities may encourage alignment of digital reporting systems to common semantic identifiers to enhance audit consistency and cross-border comparability.

Under any model, the infrastructure would not supersede national regulation.  
Policy design, materiality thresholds, enforcement mechanisms, and reporting scope would remain entirely within sovereign authority.

The infrastructure would function only as a structural substrate beneath existing obligations.

---

### 7.2 Role of Financial Free Zones

Financial free zones such as ADGM, DIFC, QFC, and others operate under internationally aligned regulatory frameworks and often serve as innovation laboratories within the region.

These jurisdictions may consider:

- Piloting semantic identifier adoption within sustainable finance rulebooks;
- Embedding structured disclosure intent identifiers within digital submission portals;
- Aligning sustainability data architecture across licensed financial institutions;
- Testing interoperability across cross-listed or cross-jurisdictional entities.

Free zones are structurally positioned to experiment with technical harmonisation without altering national regulatory sovereignty.

A phased approach beginning in free zones may allow controlled testing before broader capital market integration.

---

### 7.3 Potential Role of the GCC Secretariat

At the regional level, the GCC Secretariat may consider facilitating a structured dialogue among member-state regulators focused on technical interoperability rather than standard-setting.

Such dialogue could explore:

- Shared semantic disclosure identifiers;
- Cross-border equivalence documentation;
- Alignment of regulatory overlays without harmonising policy substance;
- Digital comparability mechanisms for capital market transparency.

This would preserve national legislative independence while enhancing technical coherence.

Importantly, this approach would not introduce a GCC-wide sustainability reporting standard.  
It would enable regional structural coordination beneath globally recognised standards already referenced by member states.

---

### 7.4 Institutional Safeguards

Any adoption pathway should include clear safeguards to ensure:

- Preservation of sovereign regulatory authority;
- Explicit separation between infrastructure stewardship and policy decision-making;
- Transparent version control and change documentation;
- Public documentation of interpretive overlays;
- Non-assertion of compliance authority.

Governance arrangements should be designed to prevent the infrastructure from evolving into a quasi-standard-setting body.

Its mandate would remain technical, neutral, and implementation-focused.

---
### 7.5 Regional Participation and Global Interoperability

The proposed infrastructure is designed to operate as a neutral technical foundation capable of supporting jurisdiction-specific regulatory frameworks while remaining interoperable across borders.

GCC jurisdictions may consider multiple levels of engagement depending on policy objectives and institutional priorities.

Participation need not imply adoption of a new standard, transfer of authority, or alteration of existing regulatory frameworks. Rather, engagement may take the form of structured technical alignment.

Possible avenues for regional engagement may include:

- Consultation in the development of jurisdiction-specific regulatory overlays.
- Alignment of national disclosure guidance with stable semantic identifiers.

Under such an approach:

- Global standards such as ISSB and GRI remain authoritative within their domains.
- The technical infrastructure functions strictly as an implementation layer beneath policy frameworks.

This model allows GCC jurisdictions to strengthen internal coherence while also positioning the region within broader global sustainability reporting developments.

By participating in structured interoperability design, GCC institutions may:

- enhance regional comparability without regulatory harmonisation mandates,
- reduce implementation divergence across advisory and software ecosystems,
- support cross-border capital market integration,
- contribute to emerging global conversations on digital sustainability reporting architecture.

Engagement may occur incrementally and proportionately, reflecting each jurisdiction’s regulatory maturity and policy priorities.

The infrastructure is therefore adaptable:

- It can reflect national_toggle requirements.
- It can document regulatory interpretation transparently.
- It can support regional coordination without imposing uniformity.

In this way, GCC participation would not represent adoption of an external framework, but contribution to a shared implementation reference layer that strengthens regulatory effectiveness while preserving institutional independence.
